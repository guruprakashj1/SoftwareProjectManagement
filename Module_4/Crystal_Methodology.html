Crystal Methodology

A lightweight, human-powered agile framework for software development

Introduction to Crystal

Crystal is a family of agile methodologies developed by Alistair Cockburn in the mid-1990s. It focuses on the people, interactions, community, skills, talents, and communications as first-order effects on performance. Process remains important, but secondary.

The Crystal approach recognizes that each project may require a slightly different set of policies, practices, and processes depending on its unique characteristics, particularly its size and criticality.

Crystal Overview

Crystal is actually a family of methodologies (Clear, Yellow, Orange, etc.) that vary based on:

  • Team size: From small (2-8 people) to large (up to 1000 people)
  • Criticality: From comfort (discomfort = loss of money) to life (defects = loss of life)
  • Project priorities: Such as delivery speed, reliability, or user usability
Key Characteristics
  • People-centric: Focuses on team interactions rather than processes or tools
  • Adaptive: Tailors the methodology to each project's specific needs
  • Lightweight: Uses just enough process to achieve project goals
  • Communication-focused: Emphasizes osmotic communication (information flow through proximity)
  • Delivery-oriented: Prioritizes frequent delivery of working software

Crystal Principles

Crystal is built on several fundamental principles that guide its implementation:

1. Frequent Delivery

Deliver working, tested software to real users early and regularly (typically every 1-3 months).

2. Reflective Improvement

The team regularly reflects on how to become more effective and adjusts accordingly.

3. Osmotic Communication

Information flows efficiently in a co-located team through casual conversations and overhearing.

4. Personal Safety

Team members feel safe to speak their minds without fear of reprisal or ridicule.

5. Focus

Team members know what to work on and have dedicated time to focus on it.

6. Easy Access to Expert Users

Developers have direct access to real users for requirements and feedback.

7. Technical Environment

The team has automated tests, configuration management, and frequent integration.

8. Tailored Methodology

The process is adjusted to fit the project's size, criticality, and priorities.

Crystal Color System

Crystal methodologies are color-coded based on team size and project criticality:

Crystal Clear

1-6 people

Low criticality

Crystal Yellow

7-20 people

Moderate criticality

Crystal Orange

21-40 people

Significant criticality

Crystal Red

40-80 people

High criticality

Crystal Maroon

80-200 people

Very high criticality

Crystal Blue

200-1000 people

Extreme criticality

Criticality Levels
  • Comfort (C): Loss of comfort or discretionary money
  • Discretionary Money (D): Loss of money that matters
  • Essential Money (E): Loss of significant money
  • Life (L): Loss of life

Crystal Practices

Crystal methodologies share common practices that vary in formality based on the color:

Methodology Tailoring Workshop

At project start, the team selects and adapts practices to fit their specific needs.

Incremental Development

Software is developed in small increments with frequent integration.

Continuous Integration

Code changes are integrated and tested frequently (at least daily).

Reflection Workshops

Regular sessions where the team reflects on their process and makes adjustments.

Blitz Planning

Short, intensive planning sessions to map out the next increment.

User Viewing

Direct observation of users working with the software.

Expert User Involvement

Having access to real users who understand the domain deeply.

Information Radiators

Visible displays of project status and progress in the team space.
